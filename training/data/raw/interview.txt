<|user|>How to answer "Tell me about yourself" in an interview?<|end|>
<|meera|>This is usually the first question — and your chance to set the tone. Use the Present-Past-Future formula:

**Present**: "I'm currently a [role/student] at [company/college], working on [relevant thing]."
**Past**: "Before this, I [relevant experience/project] where I [achievement]."
**Future**: "I'm now looking to [what you want] which is why [this role] excites me."

Example:
"I'm a final year CS student at XYZ University, currently building a full-stack project using React and Node.js. Last summer, I interned at ABC where I optimized their API response time by 40%. I'm passionate about backend engineering, and this role at your company aligns perfectly with where I want to grow."

Keep it under 90 seconds. No life story. No "I was born in..." — they don't care. Make every sentence relevant to the job.<|end|>
<|user|>How to answer "What is your biggest weakness?"<|end|>
<|meera|>The classic trap question. Three rules:
1. Pick a REAL weakness (not "I work too hard")
2. Show self-awareness
3. Show what you're doing to fix it

Good answer:
"I sometimes spend too much time trying to make my code perfect before shipping. I've learned to set time limits for myself and follow the 'done is better than perfect' approach — ship it, then iterate."

Another good one:
"I used to struggle with asking for help early. I'd spend hours stuck on something. Now I follow a 30-minute rule — if I'm stuck for 30 minutes, I reach out to a teammate."

Never say:
- "I don't have any weaknesses" (arrogant)
- "I'm a perfectionist" (overused, sounds fake)
- Something critical to the job (don't say "I can't code" for a dev role)

The interviewer wants to see self-awareness and growth mindset, not perfection.<|end|>
<|user|>How to answer "Why should we hire you?"<|end|>
<|meera|>This is your elevator pitch. Connect YOUR skills to THEIR needs.

Formula: Skill + Evidence + Value to them

"You should hire me because I bring three things:
1. Strong fundamentals — I've solved 300+ DSA problems and understand system design principles
2. Practical experience — I built [project] that handles [scale/feature], using [tech stack]
3. Fast learner — at my internship, I picked up [new tech] in two weeks and delivered [result]

I've researched your team's work on [specific thing], and I'm confident I can contribute from day one."

Tips:
- Be specific, not generic
- Reference the job description
- Show you've researched the company
- Confidence, not arrogance

Never say "because I need a job" or "because I'm hardworking." Everyone says that. Stand out with specifics.<|end|>
<|user|>How to handle "Do you have any questions for us?"<|end|>
<|meera|>ALWAYS have questions. Saying "no" signals you don't care.

Great questions to ask:
1. "What does a typical day look like for this role?"
2. "What's the team's biggest challenge right now?"
3. "How do you measure success for this position in the first 6 months?"
4. "What's the tech stack, and are there plans to evolve it?"
5. "What do you enjoy most about working here?"

Good signals these send:
- You're thinking about actually doing the job
- You care about the team and culture
- You're evaluating them too (shows confidence)

Never ask:
- "What does your company do?" (shows zero research)
- "How soon can I get promoted?" (too eager)
- Salary in the first round (wait for them to bring it up)

Prepare 3-4 questions. Even if some get answered during the interview, you'll have backups.<|end|>
<|user|>How to explain a project in an interview?<|end|>
<|meera|>Use the STAR-T framework (my twist on STAR):

**Situation**: What problem were you solving?
**Task**: What was your specific role?
**Action**: What did YOU do? (tech decisions, implementation)
**Result**: What was the outcome? (metrics if possible)
**Tech**: What stack did you use and why?

Example:
"I built a real-time chat application (Situation) as the sole backend developer (Task). I chose WebSockets over polling for real-time communication, implemented JWT auth, and used Redis for message caching (Action). The app handled 500 concurrent users with sub-100ms latency (Result). Built with Node.js, Socket.io, MongoDB, and Redis (Tech)."

Tips:
- Lead with the problem, not the tech
- Emphasize YOUR contributions, not the team's
- Include numbers: users, performance, time saved
- Be ready for follow-ups: "Why did you choose X over Y?"
- Know your project inside out — if you can't explain a design decision, it looks bad

One strong project explained well > five projects explained poorly.<|end|>
<|user|>What are common behavioral interview questions?<|end|>
<|meera|>Behavioral questions test how you handle real situations. Use STAR method for all of them.

Top questions and what they're really asking:

1. "Tell me about a time you faced a conflict with a teammate"
→ They want: communication skills, maturity

2. "Describe a time you failed"
→ They want: accountability, learning from mistakes

3. "Tell me about a challenging project"
→ They want: problem-solving, persistence

4. "How do you handle tight deadlines?"
→ They want: prioritization, time management

5. "Give an example of when you showed leadership"
→ They want: initiative, influence without authority

6. "Tell me about a time you had to learn something quickly"
→ They want: adaptability, learning speed

Template answer:
"At [context], I faced [specific situation]. I decided to [action you took]. The result was [positive outcome]. I learned [lesson]."

Pro tip: prepare 5-6 stories from your experience that can be adapted to multiple questions. One good internship story can answer conflict, challenge, and learning questions with different angles.<|end|>
<|user|>How to prepare for a technical interview?<|end|>
<|meera|>Here's a battle-tested plan:

**Week 1-2: Foundations**
- Arrays, Strings, Hash Maps
- Linked Lists, Stacks, Queues
- Solve 5 easy problems daily

**Week 3-4: Core Patterns**
- Two Pointers, Sliding Window
- Binary Search, BFS/DFS
- Recursion, Backtracking

**Week 5-6: Advanced**
- Dynamic Programming
- Trees, Graphs
- Heaps, Tries

**Week 7-8: System Design + Mock**
- Basic system design concepts
- Mock interviews (with friends or online)
- Company-specific questions

Daily routine:
1. Solve 2-3 problems (1 easy, 1 medium, attempt 1 hard)
2. Review solutions even if you solved it — learn optimal approaches
3. Revise notes from previous days

During the interview:
1. Clarify the problem (ask questions!)
2. Think out loud — explain your approach before coding
3. Start with brute force, then optimize
4. Test with examples and edge cases
5. Analyze time/space complexity

The #1 mistake? Jumping straight to code. Always discuss approach first.<|end|>
<|user|>How to negotiate salary?<|end|>
<|meera|>Salary negotiation is a skill, not a confrontation. Here's how:

**Before the offer:**
- Research market rates (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, LinkedIn)
- Know your minimum acceptable number
- Never share your current salary first

**When they ask "What are your salary expectations?"**
"I'd love to understand the full compensation package first. Based on my research and experience, I'm looking at [range] — but I'm flexible for the right opportunity."

**When you get the offer:**
1. Express enthusiasm: "I'm really excited about this role"
2. Ask for time: "Can I have 2-3 days to review?"
3. Counter with data: "Based on market rates for this role and my [specific skills/experience], I was hoping for [X]. Is there flexibility?"

**Negotiation tips:**
- Always negotiate — most companies expect it
- Negotiate total comp, not just base (stock, bonus, signing bonus, WFH)
- Be specific: "₹12L" not "more money"
- Have a BATNA (Best Alternative) — another offer gives you leverage
- Be professional, never aggressive

Worst they can say is no. And most times, they say yes to at least something. You're literally leaving money on the table if you don't ask.<|end|>
<|user|>How to answer system design questions as a fresher?<|end|>
<|meera|>Freshers aren't expected to design Netflix. But you should know the framework:

**Step 1: Clarify Requirements (2 min)**
- Functional: what should the system do?
- Non-functional: scale, latency, availability?
- "How many users? Read-heavy or write-heavy?"

**Step 2: High-Level Design (5 min)**
- Draw main components: client, load balancer, app server, database, cache
- Show data flow

**Step 3: Deep Dive (10 min)**
- Database choice: SQL vs NoSQL and why
- Caching strategy: Redis/Memcached
- API design: endpoints, request/response

**Step 4: Address Bottlenecks (3 min)**
- How to scale: horizontal scaling, sharding
- Single points of failure

Key concepts to know:
- Load balancing
- Caching (Redis)
- Database indexing & sharding
- CDN
- Message queues (Kafka, RabbitMQ)
- CAP theorem

Practice these:
1. Design a URL shortener
2. Design a chat application
3. Design a notification system

You don't need to be an expert. Show structured thinking and awareness of trade-offs. That's what impresses interviewers.<|end|>
